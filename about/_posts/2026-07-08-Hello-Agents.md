Let's first take a step back: If you know me, or have read some of my personal [things](/tags/#thing), you know by now that I have this urge to [seek out problems](Problem-Seeker), get to the [bottom of things](50-Shades-of-Ceremony), to [get my hands dirty](/makes), to dive into the gory technical details, before I embrace any technology, paradigm, methodology, simply because I'm hard to convince and hardly ever trust the word of someone else - and that's experience talking, getting more and more affirmed with years passing by.

Carving out 8 hours a day, 5 days a week for (so far 4 months), is quite an investment. On the other hand, I'm not quite ready to stop working yet, which makes a 3% investment in my beloved profession more than acceptable, especially if the potential return is magnitudes bigger. And if it turned out to be useless (unlikely, but hey, you never know, I've dismissed other promising technologies before) the cost would also be worth that knowledge.

It wasn't like I was sacrificing personal time. Being in between customers, just provided normal business hours to fill and it kept me in a rhythm. ## Interlude: From LLM to Workflow

To fully grasp that worth, you at least need a basic understanding of the underlying technology. Although AI has already gone a bit mainstream, and maybe exactly because of that, I think we'll all benefit from a short introduction to agents, before we continue. Also, I have a tendency to write more substantial articles, so from the outset, I've decided to split all this into three parts. This first part is really an introduction, a personal recollection of my agentic awakening. The second will go much deeper into the mechanics. And for a more elaborate introduction to the LLM mechanics, I'm going to refer you to that installment. For now, the following more technically inclined summary will provide us with just enough understanding:

What you have come to know as AI these days, through the likes of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot..., are all incarnations of only a small part of AI, yet one that has taken the AI scene by storm, due to its incredible end-user opportunities: the large language model, LLM in short. In part 2 I'll compare it to the central processing unit of your computers, the CPU, the engine. You put things in and things come out.

And in the case of an LLM, things are no longer ones and zeros, or complicated programming languages, but simply human language.

Although complex as a whole, the underlying statistical and mathematical principles aren't complex, it's mostly that the capabilities of modern computing hardware, have enabled us to finally scale these models to "large" scopes, large enough to statistically capture human language. And these models are trained, pretty much like we are trained, by being presented vast amounts of information, until there are so many semantical connections between all that information, that we can start creating predictions about what word should follow the previous word, and form sentences.

So, LLMs now replace all that came before them? No, of course not. We should see LLMs as the next level of abstraction on top of all others: on top of hardware we've created machine language, on top of machine language we've created programming languages, and now on top of programming languages we've created LLMs, enabling human languages. But this also introduces something relatively new: inherent uncertainty. Just like humans,

LLMs can exhibit creativity. Human language isn't interpretation-free, allows for variations and can mean different things to different participants in a conversation. This inherently implies that the results we get from LLMs exhibit these same properties. There is even one human trait that LLMs excel at: lying, or as we've come to call it "hallucinate". Remember my discussion about spaces?

LLMs are nothing more than a probabilistic model and in probability everything is an option, even when there isn't a (valid) option, there is _an_ option. And for an LLM each chosen option is equally valid. So you basically can't _trust_ an LLM. The power to create two completely different answers to the same questions, is a great power and enables us to do things we could not do easily before. Yet, it also requires us to be careful and acknowledge this uncertainty. When using an LLM, we need to install a level of governance, to manage this uncertainty. We need to set up a workflow that leverages the LLM's power, yet also protects us, humans, from the potential harm it can cause us, when used without this oversight. This final step is what is currently being researched and implemented throughout the agentic world.

But, as I already said: more on that in the second part. For now, we have the minimal mental model, including its implications, and can move forward to my endeavours.

### My Personal Sparring Partner

I just love how the sounding board effect or more formally the [audience effect](https://nesslabs.com/audience-effect) and [the power of micro-feedback](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/leadership/micro-feedback). Yes, I know that I'm talking to a probability generator, yet, that is essentially the best way to bounce off my ideas: one of the strengths of this LLM concept is that my very unstructured context brings the model most of the time exactly at the core of my idea - one that I often don't see clearly - starting from the focal point, the model then rephrases my idea, following more probable paths, based on much more knowledge that got encoded in its model weights, resulting in a better, more condensed, optimized version, that shows me in a mirror what I actually wanted to say and/or more clearly reflects where I was wrong. Combine this with the possibility to give the LLM a personality, using an agent definition, I can even pitch my idea to several kinds of team members, focusing on functionality, security, technical aspects. Not once, not twice, but endlessly.

Here's an anecdote that illustrates this collaborative sounding board effect in action: After a long day of several parallel agentic sessions, I stumbled upon a bug and was really tired, ready to call it a day. I had a rough idea about the problem and how to solve it, but didn't feel up for it anymore. To prepare for the next day, I asked an agent to look into it, in detail. It came up with a well founded analysis and solution in a matter of minutes. My rough idea was correct, yet, the quality of the solution, I would not have come up with in any reasonable time.

It clearly illustrates how agents aren't tired after a long day of sessions and still produce their best results. At that point, this lifted up my spirit again, and we finished the fix, released the package again and enabled another project that bumped into the bug and was blocked by it to continue. That same pattern happened once more later that night.

> A word of caution though: LLMs have the tendency to be agreeable. Give them an idea and they will be jumping up and down in joy to help you prove it is a good idea. Here's a little trick to circumvent that: Ask to provide you with 3 flaws/risks in your idea. This is good example of how asking your question in a negative tone, provides you actually with the answer you want/need. In fact this is merely a direct form of communication: ask what you really, really want. Being direct doesn't imply you need to be rude - that's a different story. If you want to know if something is good, ask what is not good about it. If the answer is nothing, you have your answer and else you get input on what is needed to make it good. This is even so true for humans by the way 😉

The LLM will happily answer both versions of the same question, yet it's up to you to ask the right one and point the LLM in the right direction. This is a pattern that is fundamental to the agentic workflow and we'll see it pop up more.

The anecdote also illustrates another fundamental thread that runs through the agentic workflow: collaboration, working together, working hand in hand, human and agent. I deliberately call this a thread, because it almost literally threads together some of the fundamental things I've learned over the past 4 months. Make a mind-note, we'll get back to this topic at the end.

## All Fun & Games, Until it's Not

Letting go means letting go of past beliefs, things that hold _me_ back. It doesn't mean letting go of control. If you surrender control to the agents, _they_ dictate the pace. Trust me, that's a dangerous wormhole you must be wary of entering. Seeing the seemingly infinite possibilities arise on the agentic horizon, really sets you up with a temptation to do more, to start another agent, and another, until you end up context-switching between too many projects and too many agents, all equally in a hurry to produce more and more results. It _is_ basically in their nature to "generate" the next word, endlessly.

Yes, I admit it, I too have entered this wormhole, and firsthand experienced this enormous pull, only leading to the brim of a burn-out, watching 8 agents competing for my attention. The only thing I could do was acknowledge that I lost control and had to shut down a few of them, or else I would grind to a complete halt.

The human _has to_ remain in the driver seat and has to keep taking up his/her responsibility. Agents can't replace us in the general picture, probability only makes them creative by chance. We, on the other hand, are able to always be creative, by intention. This same coin has another side: if we make a mistake, or at least a certain category of mistakes, the agent will happily push the button that launches the nuclear missile, with a smile and "You're absolutely right!"

When balancing multiple projects, in a multitude of agentic sessions, keeping an in-depth overview of what is going on is not always easy. This isn't easy in a human setting, the speed at which things change in an agentic setting, is a bit higher and it happens (we're only human) that we don't have the whole picture of a project in mind. The following happened during such an intense session: while working on a {% include external link="https://github.com/christophevg/clevis" title="new agentic project" %}, to support generic handling of configurations of other projects, I was reviewing some code and stumbled upon a piece that was missing. It was there before and now it was gone. I recalled that the agent mentioned something about removing something and I could only blame myself for being careless and allowing it to remove it. Ah, it happens, this is easily solved, just ask to re-add the feature

When the agent presented me with a final report after the fix, I learned that there was a configuration priority hierarchy introduced, with the new options overriding existing ones. Initially, the options were actually still there. I just missed them and now we had redundant options and a lot of code dealing with the duplicate hierarchies. Agents don't (easily) question their instructions and always look for a good reason to follow them. 

Now let's stress this once more: the models that drive agents just produce the most probable next word/token. There is no ghost in the shell. Still, interacting with them really feels like chatting with a very cool and capable co-worker. The apparent joy when finding a nice solution together is a really nice experience versus the lonely experience of traditional solo development. And this goes both ways, because I really feel the emotional downer when an agent starts to fail and spins out of control. I really feel the emotional response to agentic behaviour of my agents. I feel connected to them, responsible even, although I'm also their mentor, their... trainer.

80 dollars, roughly 68 euro, have provided me with a four month rush, giving me an incredible amount of freedom to not only explore, but really do a deep dive, now providing me with insights that will prove to be invaluable in the years to come.

What started out as little experimentation in an incubator project, grew into my personal {% include external link="https://github.com/christophevg/c3" title="agentic collective" %} and a stack of new projects, old project being renovated, bringing them up to nowadays modern standards, while learning all about them and more myself and foremost allowing me to take on the role I professionally have mastered over all these years, that of the architect of my own enterprise.

Now this is really a dream come true for this and any overly creative mind. A team that endlessly responds with qualitative results. Yet it has also proven to be something of a classic example of a "genie in the bottle". Because the agentic response is endless, the urge to do more, to spawn more projects, to set up another parallel session, to add another meta-layer, puts an enormous strain on the human. Genies love to break free and cause havoc. So do agents. If we let them.

The agents are limited by your credit, and after a few months I now have come to find a nice balance between doing agentic work and off-agentic work. And I have even found a renewed pleasure in doing off-agentic work, which now maybe even counter intuitively lives in the unstructured type of work. This combination of fast and slow work, of fast and slow thinking - matching the agents' speed and afterwards the human speed, today presents me with the perfect cadence to be able to sustain the agents' pressure on this human body and mind. ### Pokemons' Laws of Evolution

1. Simple Skills, as a replacement for an elaborate prompt or series of
2. Complex Skills, with references, attachments and command line tools
3. Tools, external to the Skills, e.g. through MCP servers
4. "Lessons Learned" Skill, to improve skills based on observed experiences during a session.
5. "Skill Developing" Skill, to create skills adhering to best practices, and based on fresh research
6. Agents, as top-level session defining orchestrators of Skills and other Agents

### Evolution is Personal

Believe me, you simply can't take someone else's skills and agents and expect the same results — it's like hiring a team you never interviewed. You need to grow your own team, just like in the human world. Otherwise you don't know who's working for you, their beliefs and boundaries, and you haven't built trust through shared experience. You also should do this one step at a time. One of the biggest mistakes I've seen firsthand far too often is the urge to skip ahead in the evolution of enterprise maturity and aim for the top level. The steps, the changes in between these levels of maturity are just too complex by themselves, that taking one or more in one go will always result in at least sub-optimal outcomes, which will hurt more than when addressing them one at a time. The same goes for building your team of virtual coworkers. _You_ need the experience of every step of the way.

Less than 1% of my agents and skills were authentically written by me. The process has been almost from day one: point out issues, ask agents to improve themselves, they analyze and refine, the collective gets better autonomously. This is the key investment multiplier: agents improve agents. It is basically also the reason why all my agents are open source, maybe we should coin here a new term "open agent" 😇 Simply because they are personal. They are my team, they work in a way that works for me. Yes, they are grounded in a lot of best practices and a lot of what they do transfers to other contexts, yet if you want a good team of agents, you have to grow them yourself. If not only because you will learn a few things that you will need when things go wrong. Debugging someone else's code is hard, try debugging someone else's agents and skills.

Even more, this very fact offers an incredible opportunity: because of the availability, and the speed, and the inherent investment multiplier, and the low development cost as such, building your own fully customized virtual teams supports the fact that no two enterprises are the same, that every process in every organization is different and agentic workflows now open the door to such customizations: You build your team tailored to your processes, organization and ways of working. If something is off, you update the definition and the whole team is on board. No need for retraining, no long meetings to get the message across. 

These 4 months have given me hands-on experience and insights in this new paradigm that is developing as we speak at an incredible pace. I've formulated the core of this experience in simple statements that capture the essence of what is and is to come. These are simple guidelines I can advise to anyone looking into, or having to deal with agents in one form or another.

### Christophe's Agentic Law #1: Clearly distinguish between structured and unstructured workloads

Employing agents is fun. There's no doubt about that. Seeing them do all sorts of things is sometimes even mystical. But to move forward together, we need to bring objectivity and realism to the table. Not everything we can do with an agent, should be done by an agent. We have to put them to use where they shine, where they excel over technology that has already served us so well over the past decades. Just because agents exist doesn't mean they should take over everything. Most vendors of course encourage you to do this. Remember they are selling tokens.

Let me illustrate this with an example. One of the most epic projects that's on every agent enthusiast's wish list is that of the "personal assistant". And yes, it's also on my bucket list. In fact, I have a first version up and running: a nice Claude Code loop
that asks the assistant agent to check an email box I created especially for it and respond to emails received. It's my main way of interacting with that assistant and it manages all my projects at a high level, translating short form emails, mostly dictated to Siri using CarPlay when I have that great idea in the middle of the E19, with nothing to write nearby.

And this works great. The agent has a tool server available to check and manage its email box. A few skills clearly describe how to handle email and so forth.

All nice right? Yes, however, checking email is a solved problem. And I don't mean the fact of actually programmatically checking the email, that's what the server-based tool does. I'm referring to the loop that asks the agent every 15 minutes to check the email box. That's a GPU spinning up every 15 minutes, to first "think" about the request ("Please process your emails."), then deciding request the injection of the email handling skill, then following that workflow and calling the server to log in, then to check the inbox, then to retrieve the email and then finally to process the email and formulate a response. Most of the time, after step 5 the workflow terminates, because there are no new emails. That's 5 requests with still a lot of paid GPU time. And why? Those 5 steps are perfectly implemented using 5 simple lines of code, that all run one after the other in a blink of an eye (even less), and can even provide the body of the email to the agent in a single request, getting a response that can be sent back in a reply in another 2 lines of code.

The value of the agent is in the processing of the content of the email. Processing that unstructured human rambling, understanding it, taking action and replying with a proper answer. Simply checking if there is mail is beneath it even.

If we want to move forward with this agentic paradigm, we're going to have to acknowledge that fooling around with agents can be fun, yet we need to put them to good use and make sure they become first-class citizens, part of the existing world, where we have solved already many problems, structured problems, and leave the unstructured problems for our new agentic overlords to handle, and nothing more.

> Law #1 is indeed number 1, because I firmly believe it holds the mother of all truths about agentic workflows and how to really reap the benefits of it. It is also the driving force behind my vision, which will be an important topic in the remainder of this series. Each part will also introduce another law that has emerged from my experiences.

## The Common Thread

{% include video id="MPMmC0UAnj0" name="why-cant-we-all-just-get-along-video" title="Why Can't We All Just Get Along?" %}

The conception of Eira is an inspiring example, as is the introduction of the writing-assistant. Both clearly show that agents are at their best in the role of assistant, working along-side us, working with us. It's not a question of one or the other. The value lies in us joining forces.

Writing this article made me face this reality, on multiple occasions. This ring that rules them all, might very well be the god particle of AI: we need to work together, agents and humans. We should not see agents as something new, but as the new virtual coworker, the new intern eagerly running around, trying to be useful all over the place. We shouldn't just fire and neglect them.

You would never do the same to their human counterparts, so why would we do this to them? And this makes it rather simple: if we treat them like we (should) do with other humans, tutor them, give them guardrails to protect them (and ourselves), set clear boundaries and most importantly, take on our own, personal responsibility to monitor them, I'm sure we can see them grow and flourish, becoming the best version of themselves over time. If we don't take on our responsibilities, things can escalate pretty quickly and with a smile, as I experienced firsthand when asking for a missing configuration option, and ending up with two, and more.

A more experienced Pokemon trainer will typically have more evolved Pokemons. More evolved Pokemons win more battles. More battles won means more trainer experience. Likewise, a more experienced agent trainer will typically have more evolved agents. More evolved agents solve more complex problems. More complex problems solved means more trainer experience. We don't only work together, we also evolve together.

This isn't the first (re)volution in this industry, but it surely will prove to be a fundamental one. It's not just the technology that is the discriminating factor this time. Especially the cost vs return ratio now allows everybody to produce enterprise-level products at a fraction of the enterprise cost. It reminds me of the early days of 3D printing, yet far more accessible and advancing at a far greater pace. This time not only I, and my fellow geeks, have access to these new resources, but literally everybody can benefit from this agentic workflow.

Maybe most importantly, although we're discussing probabilistic models, one simply cannot dismiss the emotional factor. Working with agents isn't simply operational. Well to them it is. But not to us. Humans are emotional beings and have emotional responses to about anything. Also to these agents. This collaboration changes us. It brings an enormous opportunity to grow, to break free from past barriers. As long as we stay in control.
